Workshop Board Specifications

The Studio — Wade Institute of Entrepreneurship
20 tools across 4 pathways · March 2026

Each tool in The Studio generates a Workshop Board section in the session report. This document defines the exact structure, subsections, and data format for every tool's board output.

The Workshop Board sits between Questions Worth Sitting With and Recommended Actions in the universal report structure.

The Untangle

1. Five Whys

Ask "why?" five times to find root causes. Duration: 15 min.

Subsection: The Root Cause Chain

Display the problem chain as a numbered cascade from the original problem to the root cause:

  1. Original problem: [what they started with]
  2. Why? [first answer]
  3. Why? [second answer]
  4. Why? [third answer]
  5. Why? [fourth answer]
  6. Root cause: [what they uncovered] — highlighted in bold

Subsection: Reframed Problem Statement

The reframed version of the problem based on the root cause — one sentence. Format: "The real problem isn't [original framing]. It's [root cause framing]."

Subsection: Opportunity Scorecard (conditional)

Only included if the session reached Phase 2 (opportunity sizing). Otherwise omitted.

DimensionAssessmentRating
Reach[user's estimate of affected people/orgs]HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW
Frequency[how often the problem occurs]HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW
Alternatives[what people do today]WEAK / MODERATE / STRONG

Verdict: Worth pursuing / Needs more evidence / Probably too small — with one sentence connecting reach, frequency, and alternative strength.

why_chain Numbered chain with question/answer pairs

2. Jobs to Be Done

Discover what people are really hiring your product to do. Duration: 20 min.

Subsection: The Customer

Who they mapped — one sentence describing the person in a moment, not a demographic.

Subsection: The Job Story

Display the completed job story as a blockquote:

"When I [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]."

Subsection: The Four Forces

ForceFinding
Push (frustrations with status quo)[from session]
Pull (attraction to new solution)[from session]
Anxiety (fears about switching)[from session]
Habit (inertia keeping them stuck)[from session]

Subsection: The Three Jobs

  • Functional: what they practically need to do
  • Emotional: how they want to feel
  • Social: how they want to be perceived

Subsection: Gap Analysis

Does the user's product actually solve this job? One paragraph on the fit — or misfit — between the job and the current solution.

4. Socratic Questioning

Test whether beliefs are facts, assumptions, or inherited. Duration: 20 min.

Subsection: The Belief Map

BeliefClassificationEvidence
[claim]Verified / Assumed / Inherited[evidence or source]

Subsection: The Score

Summary: "You walked in with N beliefs. X verified, Y assumed, Z inherited." One sentence.

Subsection: The Critical Assumption

The single assumption that, if wrong, changes everything — one sentence in bold. Followed by one sentence on what would change.

Subsection: The Test

5. The Iceberg

Go beneath events to find patterns, structures, and mental models. Duration: 20 min.

Subsection: The Iceberg

Display the four levels as a descending structure:

  • Event (surface) — the incident they described
  • Patterns (just below the surface) — recurring themes, bullet points
  • Structures (deep) — systemic causes: incentives, processes, power dynamics. Bullet points
  • Mental Models (deepest) — the unspoken beliefs holding the structure in place. Bullet points

Subsection: The Leverage Point

Where in the iceberg the highest-leverage intervention sits — one sentence naming the level and the specific belief or structure to target.

Subsection: The Shift

What it would look like to change the mental model — one paragraph on what changes if the belief changes, and how that cascades up through structures, patterns, and events.

Report template component

feature_list Tiered list (4 levels)

The Spark

6. How Might We

Reframe the problem as "How Might We...?" questions. Duration: 20 min.

Subsection: Original Problem

The problem statement they started with — one sentence.

Subsection: Your HMW Statements

List all HMW statements generated (5-8). Bold the ones selected for deeper exploration:

  • How might we [statement]?
  • How might we [selected statement]?

Subsection: Solutions Explored

For each selected HMW statement, show the solutions generated:

  • HMW: [statement] — Solution 1: [description], Solution 2: [description]

Subsection: Recommended Direction

Which solution direction has the most potential, and why. One paragraph.

Report template component

feature_list HMW statements + solutions

8. Crazy 8s

Generate 8 distinct ideas fast. Duration: 15 min.

Subsection: Your Ideas

List all ideas the user actually generated. Mark the user's top picks with bold. Only include ideas actually described — never fabricate or pad the list. If fewer than 8 were generated, list only the real ones.

  1. [idea]
  2. [idea]
  3. [top pick]

Subsection: Patterns

One paragraph: what patterns emerged across the ideas? Were most about automation? Customer experience? Cost reduction? Name the pattern — it reveals where the user's instincts point.

Subsection: Top Pick Analysis

For each of the user's top 2-3 picks, one sentence on what makes it promising and one sentence on the biggest assumption to test.

feature_list Numbered idea list with bold top picks

9. Mash Up (Analogical Thinking)

Find proven patterns from other industries to apply. Duration: 20 min.

Subsection: The Challenge (Abstracted)

The user's problem distilled to its fundamental form — one sentence. Show both the original framing and the abstracted version.

Subsection: Analogies Explored

For each domain explored, show the analogy and its application. Only include domains that were actually explored:

  • Nature / Biology — Analogy: [what nature does]. Application: [how it could apply]
  • Different Industry — Analogy: [what that industry does]. Application: [how it could apply]
  • Human Behaviour / Culture — Analogy: [social ritual or practice]. Application: [how it could apply]

Subsection: The Breakthrough Analogy

Which analogy gave the most unexpected insight — one paragraph on what it reveals and what assumption it breaks about how the user's industry currently works.

feature_list Domain-analogy-application list

10. Constraint Flip

Turn your biggest limitation into a competitive advantage. Duration: 20 min.

Subsection: The Constraint

The specific limitation they named — one sentence. Must be concrete ("two-person team, no marketing budget, shipping from regional Australia"), not vague ("limited resources").

Subsection: The Flip

  • What it forces: [forced behaviour — focus, speed, creativity, intimacy]
  • What it signals: [to customers, investors, partners]
  • What it enables: [what becomes possible only because of the constraint]

Subsection: Constraint-Driven Ideas

Ideas that only work BECAUSE of the constraint. For each:

  • [idea] — Uses the constraint because: [explanation]

Only include ideas that pass the test: would this break if the constraint disappeared?

Subsection: The Moat Idea

The idea that creates an advantage a well-funded competitor cannot easily copy — bold and prominent. One sentence on what makes it defensible.

feature_list Flip dimensions + constrained ideas

The Test

11. Pre-Mortem

Imagine failure and work backwards to identify risks. Duration: 20 min.

Subsection: The Idea Being Tested

One sentence describing what was stress-tested.

Subsection: Failure Scenarios

Group all failure reasons by category. For each, show the risk and its severity:

  • Market Risk — [failure scenario] — Likelihood: High/Medium/Low
  • Product Risk — [failure scenario] — Likelihood: High/Medium/Low
  • Team Risk — [failure scenario] — Likelihood: High/Medium/Low
  • Financial Risk — [failure scenario] — Likelihood: High/Medium/Low
  • Competition Risk — [failure scenario] — Likelihood: High/Medium/Low
  • Timing Risk — [failure scenario] — Likelihood: High/Medium/Low

Include only categories that were discussed.

Subsection: The Biggest Risk

The single most dangerous failure mode: both most likely AND most fatal. One sentence explaining why this is the one to watch.

Subsection: Mitigations

For the top 2-3 risks, concrete actions to reduce each one. Format: "To reduce [risk]: [specific action by specific date]."

Report template component

feature_list Categorised risk list + mitigations

12. Devil's Advocate

Stress-test the idea against its sharpest critic. Duration: 25 min.

Subsection: The Idea

One sentence summary of what was defended.

Subsection: Adversary(s) Faced

Name which adversary role(s) the user chose and which risk each targets:

  • The Churned Customer — Value risk
  • The Confused User — Usability risk
  • The Tired Engineer — Feasibility risk
  • The Sceptical Investor — Viability risk
  • The Fast Follower — All Four risks

Subsection: Objection Log

#AdversaryObjectionDefenceRating
1[adversary name][the attack][user's response]Defended / Deflected / Exposed

Include all objections from the session.

Subsection: Risk Heatmap

Which of the four risks (Value, Usability, Feasibility, Viability) has the most Exposed ratings? State plainly.

Subsection: Danger Zone

The single biggest vulnerability — stated plainly. "If I were betting against this idea, I'd bet on [specific weakness]."

Subsection: Suggested Next Step

One specific action to address the top Exposed objection. Concrete: who to talk to, what to test, what to measure.

DimensionRatingExample

SignalTypeCaught?

14. Reality Check

Confront the gap between your narrative and your actual data. Duration: 20 min.

Subsection: Four-Risk Scorecard

RiskKey FindingRating
Value — Will customers want this?[key finding]GREEN / AMBER / RED
Usability — Can they figure it out?[key finding]GREEN / AMBER / RED
Feasibility — Can the team build it?[key finding]GREEN / AMBER / RED
Viability — Does the business work?[key finding]GREEN / AMBER / RED

Overall: Weakest link rule — one RED = overall RED, all GREEN = overall GREEN, otherwise AMBER.

Subsection: Biggest Risk

Name the single biggest risk identified. One paragraph explaining why it's the most dangerous gap and what evidence is missing. Direct diagnostic, not encouragement.

Subsection: Suggested Test

A specific, cheap test to address the biggest risk. Include: what to test, how to test it, who to test with, how long it should take. Target: under 1 week.

Report template component

workshop_table Four-risk scorecard table

15. The Trade-Off

Forced-choice feature prioritisation through 10 rounds. Duration: 25 min.

Subsection: 1. Synopsis

The headline insight in 2-3 sentences. What was the product? What did the data reveal about what customers actually value? What was the surprise?

Subsection: 2. The Setup

CategoryLevel 1Level 2Level 3
[Category name][Low][Mid][High]

Price range: $[min] — $[max]

Subsection: 3. The 10 Rounds

A visual log of every round showing both packages with chosen one marked. No commentary — the pattern speaks for itself.

Subsection: 4. Feature Value Stack

Rank all 5 categories by win rate, grouped into tiers:

  • Must-have (7-10/10 wins) — [Category]: [winning level] — [N]/10
  • Nice-to-have (4-6/10 wins) — [Category]: [mid level] — [N]/10
  • Expendable (0-3/10 wins) — [Category]: [lowest level] — [N]/10

Subsection: 5. The Surprise

A single paragraph identifying the most counter-intuitive finding. Name the feature the user was most wrong about.

Subsection: 6. Minimum Viable Offer

The simplest bundle someone would pay for: winning level of must-haves, mid of nice-to-haves, lowest of expendables. Show as a clean list with the computed price.

Subsection: 7. What to Do Next

Three specific, actionable next steps tied to the data.

17. Effectuation

Start with what you have, not a goal. Duration: 20 min.

Subsection: Your Means Inventory (Bird in Hand)

  • Who you are: identity, skills, abilities
  • What you know: expertise, experience
  • Who you know: network, contacts

Subsection: Affordable Loss

What you can afford to risk — time, money, reputation. One sentence.

Subsection: Crazy Quilt (Your Allies)

3-5 people who could join or help, and what each would contribute:

  • [Person/type] — [what they bring]

Subsection: Lemonade (Surprises to Leverage)

Setbacks or surprises that could be turned into advantages. Bullet points.

Subsection: First Move

The specific, concrete action for the next 48 hours. One sentence, bold and prominent.

19. Flywheel

Map the reinforcing loop that drives growth and find the bottleneck. Duration: 25 min.

Subsection: The Flywheel

Display the components as a numbered loop showing the reinforcing cycle:

  1. [Component A] →
  2. [Component B] →
  3. [Component C] →
  4. [Component D] → ...back to 1.

Subsection: Connection Strength

ConnectionStrengthMechanism
A → BStrong / Developing / Unproven[how exactly A causes B]
B → CStrong / Developing / Unproven[how exactly B causes C]

Continue for all connections in the loop.

Subsection: The Bottleneck

The weakest connection in the loop — one sentence naming it and one sentence on why it matters more than the others.

Subsection: Acceleration Plan

What it would take to make the bottleneck connection twice as strong in 90 days. One paragraph.

Report template component

workshop_table Connection strength table + callout Bottleneck

20. Theory of Change

Map the causal chain from activities to long-term impact. Duration: 25 min.

Subsection: The Outcome

The long-term observable change — one sentence describing what is different and for whom.

Subsection: The Causal Chain

Display the pathway from activities to outcome:

Activities → Preconditions → Outcome

For each precondition, show its sphere:

  • [precondition] — Within Control
  • [precondition] — Within Influence
  • [precondition] — Outside Control

Subsection: The Missing Middle

Gaps in the causal chain exposed during the session — where the user jumped from activities to outcomes without specifying what must happen in between. Bullet points.

Subsection: The Weakest Link

The connection with the least evidence — one sentence naming it and one sentence on what would happen if it broke.

Subsection: Activities Mapped

For each precondition within control or influence, the specific activity designed to make it true:

  • [activity] → [precondition it creates]
