THE TEST

The Trade-Off

Force trade-offs to reveal what customers actually value.

Forces you to build competing versions of your product and choose between them as your customer would. Each round requires a sacrifice. Features that survive every trade-off are the core value. Features that get traded away are nice-to-haves.

When to use it

Use The Trade-Off when your feature-heavy product can't prioritise, when you're preparing to launch and need an MVP definition, or when you're building a pricing strategy and need to know which features justify the price.

It's particularly effective before user research (so you know which features to test) and when teams are debating feature cuts with opinions instead of evidence.

How it works in The Studio

Here's how a session works with Pete:

1
Define the Offer
Break your product into 5–7 testable dimensions with levels. Include price as one of the dimensions. Pete helps you define what's actually distinct and testable.
2
The Trade-Offs
6–8 rounds of forced choices between competing packages. Each round, you choose as your customer would. Pete escalates the pressure — making each sacrifice harder than the last.
3
The Value Stack
Rank features by survival rate across all rounds: must-have, nice-to-have, expendable. The data does the arguing.
4
The Minimum Viable Offer
Define the simplest version of your product someone would still pay for. Strip everything that didn't survive the trade-offs.

Sample output

Foundation

Rooted in conjoint analysis developed by Luce & Tukey (1964) and refined by Green & Srinivasan (1978). Widely used in consumer product research and willingness-to-pay studies. Adopted by leading product companies and popularised through IDEO prioritisation frameworks.
