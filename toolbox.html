Wade Innovation Toolbox

Twelve structured methods across four stages of innovation — from understanding the problem to developing a plan.

Clarify Not sure what's really going on? Start here.

Five Whys

~15 min

Ask "why?" five times to move past symptoms and find what's really causing the problem. From Toyota's production system, taught at Harvard Business School.

When to use

When a problem keeps recurring or your team is treating symptoms rather than root causes — before committing to a solution.

What you get

A clear root cause statement and shared team understanding of why the problem really exists.

Jobs to Be Done

~20 min

Understand what your customer or user is actually trying to accomplish. People don't adopt solutions — they hire them to make progress. Clayton Christensen's framework, used across industries from healthcare to education.

When to use

Before designing a product or service, or when your existing solution isn't gaining traction with users.

What you get

A clear "job statement" that defines what progress your user or customer is really trying to make.

Empathy Map

~20 min

Deeply understand your user before defining the problem — what they say, think, do, and feel. The gap between those is where the real opportunity lives. Stanford d.school's Empathise stage.

When to use

Early in your design process, when you need to understand a specific user group before defining the problem you're solving.

What you get

A shared map of your user's perspective — what they say, think, do and feel — and the gaps that reveal real opportunities.

Ideate Have a direction? Generate bold ideas before settling on one.

How Might We

~20 min

Reframe your problem as an opportunity and open up new directions before committing to one. Stanford d.school's bridge between defining a problem and generating solutions.

When to use

After you've defined a problem and want to open up solution space before jumping to answers — best used after an empathy exercise.

What you get

A set of "How Might We" questions that reframe your challenge as a design opportunity.

SCAMPER

~20 min

A structured checklist for generating ideas: Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to other uses, Eliminate, Reverse. Alex Osborn's creativity method, widely used in product design.

When to use

When you have an existing product, service or process you want to improve — or when brainstorming feels stuck.

What you get

A range of new ideas generated by systematically challenging each element of your current approach.

Crazy 8s

~15 min

Generate 8 distinct ideas fast. Forces quantity over quality and breaks your fixation on the first "good" idea. The cornerstone of Google Ventures' Design Sprint methodology.

When to use

When you need to move fast, break creative blocks, or push your team beyond the first "safe" idea.

What you get

Eight distinct concepts in eight minutes — breadth over depth, quantity over quality.

Validate Have an idea? Stress-test it before you commit.

Pre-Mortem

~20 min

Imagine your initiative didn't land. Work backwards to surface the risks you'd miss from an optimistic mindset. Research shows it increases risk identification by 30%.

When to use

Before committing significant resources to an initiative — especially when your team is overly confident about a plan.

What you get

A prioritised list of risks and failure scenarios — and the actions needed to prevent them.

Devil's Advocate

~25 min

Stress-test your idea against its sharpest critic. Best used when your team is too aligned — or you need to find the holes before a major decision or investment of resources.

When to use

When your team is too aligned, or before a major decision, pitch or significant investment of resources.

What you get

A sharper, more defensible idea — with key objections surfaced and addressed before they matter.

Rapid Experiment

~20 min

Design the cheapest, fastest test that would kill your riskiest assumption. Validate before you build — the core principle behind Lean Startup.

When to use

When you have a risky assumption that could invalidate your idea — before you invest in building the full solution.

What you get

A clear experiment design with success criteria — and real-world data to inform your next move.

Develop Idea validated? Now map out how you'll make it real.

Lean Canvas

~30 min

Map the key elements of your initiative on one page. Start with the problem, not the solution — then surface the assumptions you're least certain about.

When to use

When developing a new business model, product or initiative and you need to align your team on the core assumptions.

What you get

A single-page business model with key assumptions clearly mapped — and the most uncertain ones flagged for testing.

Effectuation

~20 min

Don't start with a goal — start with what you already have. Expert entrepreneurs build from their own skills, network, and resources. By Saras Sarasvathy.

When to use

When you're resource-constrained, operating in uncertainty, or want to move faster by starting with what you already have.

What you get

A clear picture of your current means — who you are, what you know, who you know — and a set of realistic next steps.

Analogical Thinking

~20 min

Borrow solutions from other domains. How did nature solve this? How did another industry handle it? The technique behind many of history's most disruptive innovations — used by IDEO and DARPA.

When to use

When you're stuck on a problem that feels unique, or when conventional solutions aren't working.

What you get

Transferable insights from analogous domains — and a fresh perspective on your own challenge.
