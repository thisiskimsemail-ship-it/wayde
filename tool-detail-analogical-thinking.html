THE SPARK

Analogical Thinking

Borrow solutions from unexpected places.

Solving a problem by drawing on how a similar problem has been solved in a completely different domain. Most challenges have been addressed somewhere — in nature, another industry, a different culture or era. Shinkansen nose from kingfisher beak. Velcro from burs. Airbnb's trust model from eBay.

When to use it

Use when conventional approaches are exhausted, when the team keeps generating similar ideas, or when you want a genuinely differentiated solution. Also when a problem feels unique — it usually isn't.

Core tool in The Spark for finding proven patterns.

How it works in The Studio

Here's how a session works with Pete:

1
Define the core challenge
What specific problem needs solving?
2
Abstract the underlying pattern
What type of problem is this, stripped of context?
3
Search for analogies
Pete prompts across domains — nature, military, healthcare, sports, etc.
4
Map the parallels
What's the mechanism that made the analogy work?
5
Select strongest analogy
Which one transfers most cleanly?
6
Translate to your context
How would you apply this mechanism to your challenge?

Sample output

Here's what an Analogical Thinking session looks like in practice:

Example: Reducing Customer Support Wait Times
Your problem: "Customers wait too long for support, then leave."
Abstract pattern: "Queue management under variable demand."
Healthcare
Emergency room triage — don't serve in order, serve by urgency.
Transferable: Priority-based routing.
Airlines
Self-service kiosks handle 80% of check-ins.
Transferable: AI handles simple queries, humans handle complex.
Restaurants ★
Appetisers while you wait — the wait becomes part of the experience.
Transferable: Serve quick-win content while they're in queue — relevant help articles, video walkthroughs, community answers.
Selected Analogy
The restaurant "appetiser" model — transform dead waiting time into productive self-service time. If 40% of issues resolve before an agent connects, you've cut queue length without adding headcount.

What you get

Set of analogies from other domains, each suggesting a direction or mechanism. Session ends by selecting the most generative analogy and translating its solution into a concrete experiment or design direction.

Foundation

Rooted in classical rhetoric (Aristotle). Modern application by IDEO's biomimicry practice and DARPA. Used by P&G's Connect + Develop program. Connected to Clayton Christensen's disruptive innovation research.
